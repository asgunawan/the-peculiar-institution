## The Full Colonial Tobacco Process (Baseline: Best Chesapeake Practice, ~1650–1780)

Understanding the complete optimal process is necessary to know what "skipping steps" looks like at lower tiers.

### 1. Seedbed Preparation (January–February)
- 40 square yards of seedbed per acre of crop intended
- Site cleared, burned, and hoed
- Tiny seeds mixed with sand for even distribution; sown before mid-March
- Beds covered with pine boughs to protect emerging seedlings from frost
- Seedlings thinned to ~4 inches apart after a month
- Beds fertilized with wood ash or animal manure

### 2. Field Preparation & Transplanting (May)
- Fields prepared into knee-high hills, one every 3–4 feet
- **Most arduous task**: an experienced adult could make at most 500 hills per day
- Planting waited for rain to soften soil; tobacco peg (curved wood or deer antler) used to make hole
- Not all transplants survived; hills often replanted multiple times
- Weekly hoeing and hand cultivation until plants were knee-high (suppresses weeds and cutworms)

### 3. Priming, Topping, and Suckering (mid-summer)
- **Priming**: remove the 2–4 lowest leaves (sand lugs — dirty, heavy, close to ground)
- **Topping**: remove the compact flower cluster at the plant's crown. This forces energy into leaves. Without it, the plant wastes resources on flowers and seeds → smaller leaves.
- Post-topping height: 3–4 feet
- **Suckering**: after topping, the plant sprouted suckers at every leaf-stem junction. These had to be removed *weekly* by hand. Missed suckers → smaller leaves, lower yield.
- Leaves removed varied by soil fertility, tobacco variety, and season

### 4. Pest Management (continuous, June–September)
- Most feared pest: the **hornworm** (same species that attacks tomatoes), could grow "to the size of a man's finger"
- A hornworm plague could destroy an entire crop in under a week
- Planters inspected each tobacco plant **daily** during peak worm season (two peaks per summer)
- Worms picked off by hand and crushed underfoot
- This is pure labor intensity — no chemical solution in 1780

### 5. Harvest (late August–early September)
- Plants stood 6–9 feet tall at maturity
- Judgment call by the experienced cropmaster: color (yellowish-green), texture (thick, rough, downy), pliancy (a leaf that broke when folded)
- **Timing risk**: harvesting too early → worth less; too late → frost kills everything
- Cut between lowest leaves and ground with a sharp knife
- If weather was favorable: left on ground **3–4 hours to wilt** → heavier, moister leaf → higher price
- Field was visited multiple times as plants ripened at different rates

### 6. Curing (4–6 weeks)
- Early method (pre-1618): simply covered with hay and left in the field to sweat — abandoned by regulation
- Next method: hung on lines or sticks, first outside on fence rails
- **Tobacco barns** in use by the 1620s; by 1780 these are standard
- **Fire-cured** was the dominant Virginia method: hardwood fires kept on continuous or intermittent low smoulder inside the barn; 3 days to 10 weeks depending on process
- Color changed from greenish-yellow to light tan during curing
- **Mold** was a constant danger during this period
- "**Striking**": experienced judgment call on when the tobacco was ready to come down

### 7. Sweating and Sorting
- After striking, leaves laid on the barn floor to sweat for 1–2 weeks
- Logs used to press tobacco and raise temperature, but too much heat → mold spoils crop
- **"In case"** condition: the ideal moisture balance. Tobacco stretches like leather, glossy and moist. Too damp → rots in transit; too dry → crumbles, unsalable.
- Leaves tied into **"hands"** of 5–14 leaves
- Hands returned to platforms to sweat again; then inspection and final grading

### 8. Prizing (Packing into Hogsheads)
- Leaves pressed ("prized") into standardized hogshead barrels
- Hogshead: 48 inches long, 30 inches diameter; ~1,000 lbs fully packed with tobacco
- Early export alternative: twisted/rolled into rope, wound into balls up to 100 lbs in canvas or barrels — still used until bulk export ban in 1730
- Hogsheads transported by rolling overland or by river barge to shipping docks

## Tier Framework

### Tier 1 — Ignorant/Frontier Practice
*"I heard tobacco grows in Virginia. I have seeds and land."*

**What they do (or skip):**
- Seeds scattered directly into rough ground — no dedicated seedbeds, no fertilization, no frost protection
- No systematic hill-making; plants grow more or less in place
- No priming, no topping → plants flower and seed freely → energy lost → leaves half the expected size
- No suckering → additional yield loss
- No daily pest inspection → hornworm plagues go unchecked → crop can be wiped out mid-season
- Harvest timing is guesswork (no cropmaster judgment)
- Curing: stacked under hay or in a rough shed (pre-barn method), not fire-cured properly
- No proper sweating, no "in case" quality sorting
- Packed loosely in barrels or as rope-bundles

**Results:**
- Highly variable yield, often 30–50% of Tier 3 per worker
- Low-quality leaf, sells for bottom of market price
- High risk of total crop loss from hornworms or bad curing
- Rapid soil depletion (no rotation or fallow knowledge)

**Game feel:** Starting condition. Reflects the ignorance of a new planter who has land and workers but hasn't learned the craft. Every season is a gamble.

**Historical note:** This roughly mirrors Jamestown's earliest tobacco years before John Rolfe (who brought *Nicotiana tabacum* seeds from Trinidad c.1612) and before planters learned the full cultivation cycle. The NPS source explicitly says "in the first few years... the settlers paid little heed to quality control."

---

### Tier 2 — Standard Colonial Practice
*"I know how it's done. I do what my neighbors do."*

**What they add over Tier 1:**
- Proper seedbeds (cleared, burned, pine-bough covered, seed-and-sand sowing)
- Hill-making in prepared fields; transplanting after rain
- Consistent priming and topping
- Periodic suckering (but not necessarily weekly or complete)
- Periodic worming (not daily inspection — some losses still happen)
- **Tobacco barn** built and used for fire-curing (4–6 weeks, hardwood smoke)
- Basic sorting into hands; basic sweating
- Hogshead packing

**Results:**
- Moderate, consistent yield (~60–70% of optimal per worker)
- Marketable quality leaf
- Occasional hornworm losses in bad summers
- Still rapid soil depletion from monoculture, but crop doesn't fail catastrophically

**Game feel:** This is a stable but pressured operation. Income is real but soil degrades faster than you can expand. You're making money but you're on a treadmill.

**What gets you here:**
- Building a tobacco barn (infrastructure cost)
- Learning from experience (time/seasons played, or a one-time knowledge unlock)

---

### Tier 3 — Chesapeake Expert Practice
*"My grandfather taught me this. Every step is done right."*

**What they add over Tier 2:**
- **Daily hornworm inspection** — eliminates catastrophic worm-loss events
- **Weekly complete suckering** — full leaf development, maximum leaf size per plant
- **Expert harvest timing** — the cropmaster's judgment: color, texture, pliancy. Willingness to wilt in field 3–4 hours for heavier, higher-price leaf.
- **Controlled curing** — read mold risk, know exactly when to strike
- **Full "in case" processing** — proper sweating, perfect moisture balance, no transit losses
- **Soil rotation awareness** — knows which plots are spent, rotates to fresh land or leaves depleted ground fallow
- **Variety/quality separation** — separates sand lugs from mid-leaf from ligero, grades for market price optimization

**Results:**
- Near-optimal yield (~1,700 lbs/worker-year Chesapeake benchmark, or near it)
- Higher average sell price (better quality grades)
- Dramatically lower catastrophic loss risk
- Slower soil depletion (fallow/rotation practice)

**Game feel:** You've mastered the craft. The pressure now comes from structural forces — prices, debt, needing more land as old soil runs out. This is the ceiling of knowledge; further gains come from labor and capital investment (more workers, more plots, overseer system).

**What gets you here:**
- The NPS source says this knowledge was passed "at their fathers' knees" — it's generational expertise
- In game terms: T2→T3 could be a knowledge adoption rather than a capital purchase (perhaps requiring a full year at T2, or a "study the craft" action)
- OR this is what hiring an experienced overseer represents: the overseer *is* the T3 knowledge

## Tier Differentiation Summary

| Factor | Tier 1 | Tier 2 | Tier 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seedbed practice | None | Proper seedbeds | Proper + fertilized |
| Transplanting | Rough scatter | Hills, peg transplant | Hills, peg, timed to rain |
| Topping | Skipped | Done | Done precisely |
| Suckering | Skipped | Periodic | Weekly, complete |
| Worming | Skipped | Periodic | Daily inspection |
| Harvest timing | Guesswork | Experienced | Expert (color+texture+pliancy) |
| Wilting | Skipped | Sometimes | Always (when weather permits) |
| Curing facility | Hay/rough shed | Tobacco barn | Tobacco barn, managed fires |
| Quality control | None | Basic hands | Full "in case" grading |
| Soil awareness | None | Some rotation | Active fallow rotation |
| **Yield (relative)** | **~35%** | **~65%** | **~100%** |
| **Quality price modifier** | **0.6–0.7x** | **0.85–0.95x** | **1.0x** |
| **Catastrophic loss risk** | **High (worms, curing)** | **Low-medium** | **Low** |

## Game Mechanic Implications

### T1 → T2: Capital Upgrade
- Requires building a **Tobacco Barn** (construction cost, built in Winter)
- Barn is a prerequisite for fire-curing; without it, cured tobacco quality is capped
- Could also gate on a "seedbed preparation" action being available

### T2 → T3: Knowledge Upgrade
- Does NOT require purchasing anything — it's practice and judgment
- Could represent:
  - A passive unlock after N seasons at T2 ("you've learned from experience")
  - A direct cost of hiring someone who knows (early overseer sketch)
  - A "study/apprentice" action that costs worker-seasons
- This is historically accurate: Breen's *Tobacco Culture* emphasizes that Chesapeake planters treated this knowledge as cultural identity, passed generationally

### Soil Health Integration
- T1 plots: soil depletion penalty is **fastest** (no rotation, exhausted quickly)
- T2 plots: standard depletion rate (current game model)
- T3 plots: depletion is **slower** (rotation and fallow practices)
- This creates a meaningful long-term incentive to invest in T3 even if short-term yields look fine at T2

### Yield Modifier Mapping to `yieldModifier`
The current `yieldModifier` field (0.3–1.0) on plots could be extended or supplemented by a per-plot or global **knowledge tier multiplier**:
- T1: applies a 0.5–0.6 cap to effective yield even on good soil
- T2: standard (current model)
- T3: adds a 1.1–1.15 multiplier above base, but requires the daily labor investment (worming consumes worker-time)

## Open Design Questions

1. **Is tier per-plot or global?** Likely global (you either know how to top/worm or you don't), but the barn is a physical thing per-plot or per-operation.
2. **Does worming consume workers?** Daily inspection is real labor. Could model as a new assignment type ("worming") that costs workers but prevents worm-event yield loss. Alternatively, absorb into "tending."
3. **Does wilting count as a harvest sub-step?** The 3–4 hour wilt for heavier leaf could be a binary choice at harvest: "rush harvest" vs. "careful harvest" with a yield modifier trade-off.
4. **Seedbed labor cost:** NPS says 40 sq yds per acre, cleared/burned/hoed. This is real Winter/Spring labor. Should T2 seedbeds consume a meaningful worker-season chunk in Spring setup?
5. **Curing barn as bottleneck:** Historical operations had finite barn space. One barn = finite sticks = finite crop throughput per season. Could cap how many plots you can fully cure. This creates a capital expenditure ladder: barn #1, barn #2, etc.
6. **What does the planter-as-cropmaster represent mechanically?** In 1780 the planter himself was often the T3 knowledge node. When does that transition to needing an overseer (T3 knowledge at scale)?
