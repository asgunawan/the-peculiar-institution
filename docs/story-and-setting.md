# The World of The Peculiar Institution — Story & Setting Bible
*Grounding document: who you are, where you live, what it looks like, how it works.*
*For educational tone-setting and narrative design. Not a mechanics spec.*

---

## The Year

**1780.** The Revolutionary War is still being fought. The British have closed American ports to normal trade; the tobacco market is disrupted. French merchants are stepping in as intermediaries, but credit is tight, prices are uncertain, and every planter in Virginia is nervous. Nobody knows yet if this country will survive.

This timing is not incidental. Virginia's tobacco planters — Washington, Jefferson, Henry, Madison — were the loudest voices for independence, and a major reason was **debt**. Most of them owed enormous sums to British merchant-factors, and they resented it. The Revolution was partly a chance to reset that ledger. Your character is living inside that pressure.

---

## Who You Are

**You are William Calder, age 28. Younger son of a Tidewater planting family.**

Virginia inheritance customs followed primogeniture loosely: the eldest son gets the family estate. Your older brother James inherited your father's plantation in Westmoreland County — the house, the fields, the established workers, the factor relationship. You got a cash legacy of £400, six enslaved workers, and your father's advice: *"Go west. The Tidewater is finished. The Piedmont still has good land."*

So you did. You bought 480 acres of Piedmont land in what is now Albemarle County — rolling hills, a creek with a good landing, second-growth forest and three cleared fields. The land cost £180 plus a credit line with a Glasgow merchant house, Buchanan & Co., who expect repayment in hogsheads of cured tobacco over three to five years.

You are not wealthy. You are not a gentleman of leisure. You are a man with a small operation, a debt you can't quite see the bottom of, and a window of time before the soil and the ledger force you to make hard decisions.

**What your family taught you:** The broad strokes of tobacco farming — when to plant, what a ready leaf looks like, why you must top. You watched your father do it. You did not, however, run your own operation before. There is a difference between watching and doing.

**What you don't have:** An overseer. Established factor relationships of your own. Enough workers to tend all your cleared land simultaneously. Experience managing a gang. Spare money.

**Historical note on this backstory:** This scenario — younger son moves to Piedmont with small capital and a few enslaved workers, buys land on credit — is documented extensively in the 1770s–1790s Virginia records. It is not a cliché. It is the historical mean.

## Why Not "Inherited a Rundown Farm" (The Stardew Version)?

The Stardew framing (grandparent leaves you a neglected farm) maps poorly onto 1780 Virginia for one reason: **land in Virginia was not abandoned, it was exhausted.**

A Tidewater farm left idle didn't become a "fixer-upper" — it became scrubland and gully-washed clay. There was no sentimental journey back to childhood summers. Exhausted Tidewater planters sold out and moved west, or diversified into wheat, or went bankrupt. A neglected Tidewater tobacco farm was worth almost nothing because the soil could not grow another decade of tobacco.

The Piedmont fresh-start is more honest: you aren't restoring something, you are building something from scratch on borrowed time and borrowed money. The clock is the debt. The pressure is real.

## The Physical Layout of Your Farm

What does it actually look like when you ride up the lane in 1780?

### The Planter's House
Not a mansion. Not yet, maybe never. A two-story frame house, painted white if paint could be had, unpainted weathered grey if not. Four rooms: a hall (which doubles as dining room and receiving room), a parlor, two bedrooms upstairs. An attic where a visiting factor or a neighbor might sleep. The furniture is functional — a good table, a few Windsor chairs, a desk for the account books, a sideboard. A fireplace in every room.

**Separate from the house:** A detached kitchen (all plantation kitchens were detached — fire risk and heat). A smokehouse, about 8x8 feet, where pork and beef were hung and cold-smoked for winter stores. A root cellar or spring house for dairy and vegetables.

The house sits on a slight rise. From the front porch you can see down to the fields.

### The Quarter
Between the house and the fields: a row of **one or two small log or frame cabins**, each roughly 16 by 20 feet, one room with a dirt floor and a single stone hearth. Sleeping pallets on the floor. Perhaps a loft sleeping area for children. A small kitchen garden behind each cabin — this is the enslaved workers' subsistence garden, tolerated and sometimes encouraged because it reduced the planter's obligation to feed them.

At the start of your operation: one cabin for your six workers. There is no separate cabin for a family; the space is shared.

**Historical note:** Quarter cabins in this period were rough. They improved over time if the planter was prosperous and saw it as in his interest. They were not private. They were crowded. This is documented in archaeological work at dozens of Virginia plantation sites.

### The Tobacco Barn
The largest building on the farm. A good tobacco barn was 30–40 feet long and 20 feet wide — bigger than the planter's house. It had **ventilating slots** (gaps between boards, or hinged boards that could be opened and closed) to control airflow during curing. Inside: tier poles running the length of the barn at 4-foot vertical intervals from floor to ceiling, on which tobacco sticks were hung. A fire pit in the center floor for hardwood fire-curing.

Without a proper tobacco barn, you cannot properly cure tobacco. This is a real capital investment.

**At game start:** You have one barn. It is sufficient for a small operation. A second would let you cure more volume simultaneously.

### The Fields
Cleared, broken up into several distinct areas separated by tree lines or fence rails. Tobacco fields have a characteristic look during summer: rows of knee-high dirt hills, each topped by a leafy plant that will reach 6 feet by harvest. Corn grows in the fields between tobacco rotations (corn was essential for feeding workers and livestock).

**The land around the fields** is a mix of:
- Second-growth scrub where old cleared fields are resting/reverting
- Uncleared forest (future expansion, if you have the labor to clear it)
- A creek or branch with a small landing/dock where hogsheads are loaded onto barge or small boat

### The Landing
If your land is on a navigable waterway (and most Piedmont farms of this period sought creek access), there is a small **warehouse or tobacco shed** at the landing where finished hogsheads wait for pickup. The factor's agent comes by barge or boat, inspects the hogsheads, and takes them downriver to the main town warehouse (Richmond, or whatever entrepôt is nearest). There they are graded, priced, and shipped.

You do not set the price. The factor sets the price. This is important.

## How the Money Actually Works (The Factor System)

This is probably the most important thing to understand about a Virginia tobacco planter's life and it is almost entirely absent from popular history.

**You do not sell your tobacco.** You *consign* it.

The process:
1. You fill your hogsheads and roll them to the landing.
2. They go downriver to your factor's warehouse.
3. Your factor (in Glasgow or London, or increasingly Richmond) sells the tobacco on your behalf when market conditions are favorable.
4. The proceeds are credited to your account.
5. Against that credit, you order goods: tools, cloth, salt, sugar, hardware, occasionally enslaved workers at auction.
6. If your credit runs out before you get goods, you borrow — at interest — against next year's crop.

**The trap:** Tobacco prices were set in London. Planting decisions were made in Virginia six months earlier. You had no information advantage. You grew tobacco, priced it in a market you couldn't see, ordered goods priced at whatever your factor charged (often inflated), and paid interest on the gap. Many planters were permanently in debt to their factors. Jefferson died in debt. Washington pivoted to wheat partly to escape this system.

**In game terms:** The factor relationship is the background financial pressure. You don't see the factor onscreen, but you feel his presence every time the sell price drops or interest eats into your reserves.

## The People on Your Farm

### The Enslaved Workers (your starting six)

Not abstractions. Not "units." Six human beings, brought with you from your family's estate or purchased at auction in Richmond before you left. By 1780, Virginia had a large and growing enslaved population — the domestic slave trade was already active.

They have skills and histories. Some will have tobacco experience; some may not. Your operation does not work without them, and your relationship with them is the central moral fact of the game.

**Historical population note (1780 Virginia):** Virginia had roughly 250,000 enslaved people at this time — nearly 40% of the total population. The institution was pervasive, legally defined, economically foundational, and already generating internal conflict in Virginia society (some elite planters, including Washington and Jefferson, expressed private discomfort while continuing to hold people in bondage).

The game's title is not ironic. The "peculiar institution" was what antebellum southerners called slavery when they wanted to talk about it without saying the word.

### The Planter (You)
You manage the operation. You make assignments. You decide when to plant, when to harvest, what to sell. In the early game, you are also the *cropmaster* — the person who actually knows what a ready leaf looks like. As your operation grows, that knowledge either stays with you or you need to delegate it.

In this period, many small planters worked alongside their enslaved workers during peak seasons (transplanting, harvest). The "gentleman farmer who never got his hands dirty" is more accurate for the elite. You are not elite yet.

### No Overseer (Yet)
An overseer was a hired white man who managed daily field labor so the planter could attend to accounts, factor relations, politics, and society. Small planters couldn't afford one or didn't need one. As operations scaled past 15–20 workers, an overseer became necessary. This is a meaningful game upgrade with real historical and moral weight — it inserts a layer of coercion between you and your workers.

## The Wider World (Context, not mechanics)

- **Richmond** is the nearest major town; established in 1737, it became Virginia's capital in 1780 (the very year the game starts).
- **The French alliance** (1778) has opened some alternative credit and trade routes, but the tobacco market is still depressed.
- **Soil exhaustion** is already a known and discussed problem. Piedmont planters are working fresh soil; Tidewater planters are already desperate or pivoting.
- **1793: The Cotton Gin** is invented by Eli Whitney. This changes everything for the South — but cotton's center of gravity is further south (Georgia, Carolinas, later Mississippi). Virginia planters who survive can adapt. This is the game's victory horizon: build a stable enough operation that you can survive the tobacco price collapse and position for what comes next.

## Design Implications

### Educational hook
The game should teach:
- Why Virginia planters went into revolutionary politics (debt, factor system)
- How tobacco cultivation actually worked (labor intensity, knowledge requirements)
- The fundamental moral horror of making all this work on enslaved labor
- Why the tobacco economy was ecologically unsustainable

### Tone
Not a power fantasy. Not a condemnation lecture. A *portrait*: here is how a man in this time and place thought about his life, his land, his debt, and the people he held in bondage. The player sees the logic of the system from the inside. That is more unsettling and more educational than an external moral judgment.

### Starting state narrative
You arrive with:
- 480 acres (three cleared fields, the rest forest/scrub)
- Six enslaved workers
- One tobacco barn
- One small house
- One quarter cabin
- £220 cash on hand (after land purchase)
- £160 in debt to Buchanan & Co., due in tobacco over 3 years
- It is Spring, 1780. The seeds are ready. The hills need making.

### The name
"The Peculiar Institution" — Southerners' own euphemism for slavery, widely used from the 1830s onward. Using it as the game title is deliberately uncomfortable. The player is running the peculiar institution. They will feel its logic from the inside. The discomfort is the point.
