Eli Whitney's cotton gin allowed southern planters to dramatically expand cotton production for national and international markets, while water-powered textile factories in England and the American Northeast rapidly turned raw cotton into cloth.[7] Nearly all the exported cotton was shipped to Great Britain, fueling its burgeoning textile industry and making the powerful British Empire increasingly dependent on American cotton and southern slavery.[1]

The honest truth is that no game currently models this specific full chain in a historically grounded antebellum American South setting. Victoria 3 is the closest in terms of the economic and political context, and it's genuinely a deep game if that's the kind of thing you're after. It actually engages with slavery as a systemic economic problem with real political blowback rather than just ignoring it or making it a joke.

Great question, and this is actually one of history's most fascinating "butterfly effect" stories. The short version: cotton itself is ancient, but the specific American slave-cotton machine was basically born in 1793 from a single invention.

Cotton is NOT new at all

Cotton has been cultivated and spun into cloth for thousands of years — India, Egypt, China, Mesoamerica all had independent cotton traditions going back millennia. So cotton + humans has always been a thing. But the American plantation + cotton + slavery triangle? That's surprisingly recent and hinges on one specific moment.

Before cotton: tobacco and rice were king

In the years before the Civil War, American planters in the South continued to grow Chesapeake tobacco and Carolina rice as they had in the colonial era.[6] Slavery already existed, but it was being used for those crops. Here's the twist though — at the end of the 18th century, the southern economy was actually faltering. Existing slave labor was used to grow the traditional crops of the south such as tobacco, indigo, cotton, and rice, none of which were particularly profitable at the time. Some plantation owners even began to question whether they really needed slaves — the upkeep of owning enslaved people was not justified by the profits they were receiving.[2]

So ironically, slavery was on potentially shaky economic ground before cotton exploded.

The one invention that changed everything: the cotton gin (1793)

Many wished to convert their farms to grow cotton because England, having recently developed new machines to process cotton into cloth, would buy as much as southerners could grow. But separating the fragile cotton fibers from the seed — a process known as ginning — was too time consuming and costly to make a profit on the crop.[1]

While an enslaved person needed about ten hours to separate the seeds from one pound of cotton fiber by hand, two people using the cotton gin could produce about fifty pounds of cotton in the same timeframe.[1]

So the bottleneck wasn't growing cotton, it was processing it. Whitney's machine obliterated that bottleneck overnight.

Here's the truly wild irony: this inadvertently led to an increase in the use of slaves. Whitney had hoped his invention would do the opposite — by reducing the amount of labor needed to process cotton — but he never invented a machine to harvest cotton. That job still had to be done by hand. Cotton harvesting machines did not show up until the 1930s.[4]

So he solved the processing problem, which made cotton so profitable that planters massively scaled up fields — which needed way more enslaved hands to pick. The number of enslaved people rose with the increase in cotton production, from 700,000 in 1790 to over three million by 1850.[1]

The economic explosion that followed

Planters and their enslaved workers raised the cash crop to meet demand from British and northern textile factories. Within only a decade, by 1805, cotton production rapidly increased from two million pounds to more than sixty million pounds.[3]

By 1860, the region produced two-thirds of the world's cotton.[3] And by 1860, the South's investment in its slaves actually exceeded in value all its other investments, including land.[1]

So to directly answer your question: no single person sat down and said "let's use slaves for cotton." It was more of an economic cascade — cotton was always there but unprofitable to process, one machine made it insanely profitable in 1793, and the entire Southern economy reorganized itself around scaling that profit as fast as possible using the labor system that already existed. The "who's idea was it" is really Eli Whitney's, but completely accidentally and against his own intentions.

For your game, this is a genuinely interesting design space — the player would start before the cotton gin era (maybe 1780s) growing tobacco with existing slaves, then the gin gets invented and you have a choice: scale up and go full cotton economy, or... don't. The moral weight builds naturally through pure economic logic rather than being told it's bad.

This is where it gets genuinely fascinating from a pure game design and economics standpoint. The honest answer is: free labor wasn't just slightly more expensive — it was roughly twice as expensive, and you'd also be fighting a structural disadvantage that compounds over time. Here's why:

The raw cost difference

Despite rising purchase prices for slaves during the 19th century, the cost of hiring slave labor was consistently lower than the cost of hiring free labor throughout the period. A master in the South on average might have had to pay twice as much for hiring a free laborer than for hiring a slave laborer, even after including subsistence and surveillance costs for slaves.[1]

So in pure spreadsheet terms, your labor line item immediately doubles if you go the ethical route.

The productivity problem on top of that

It's not just the wage cost — it's also output per worker. A plantation using gang labor could produce on average 39% more output from comparable inputs than free farms or farms employing non-gang labor. The secret of success was that slaves could be driven, whereas free labor could not.[6]

So you're paying 2x the labor cost AND getting less output per worker. That's a brutal double penalty in spreadsheet terms.

If slaves could furthermore be coerced to work longer or harder hours than free laborers — as much research suggests — this would be a further bonus adding to how relatively cheap slave labor could be.[1]

The market-level trap

Here's the really nasty part for your hypothetical ethical capitalist: even if you personally chose free labor, the market price of cotton was being set by competitors who weren't paying wages. It is not surprising that there were few free wage laborers in the antebellum South's cotton fields, and that so many immigrants avoided the Southern states — having to compete with such cheap laborers put downward pressure on the wage levels for anyone voluntarily seeking work in the antebellum South.[1]

Meaning the cotton market price was essentially structured around slave labor costs. You'd be selling cotton at a price that assumed your competitor has near-zero labor costs, while you're paying full wages. You'd be getting squeezed from both sides.

The upfront capital vs. ongoing cost tradeoff

There's one angle where free labor looks better on paper: slaves were an enormous upfront capital cost. During the antebellum period, the value of a slave in today's dollars ranged from $60,000 (in 1809) to $184,000 (in 1859).[3] That's a massive capital investment with risk — a slave could die, escape, or get injured.

A free labor model avoids that capital risk. But the problem is the ongoing operational cost swamps that advantage pretty quickly given the 2x wage differential.

So for your game, the "no slaves" run would look like:

~2x your labor costs on every plantation tile
~39% lower output per worker
Selling into a market where your cotton price is determined by competitors with near-zero labor costs
You could theoretically survive by being extremely efficient in other parts of the chain (transportation, ginning, selling directly to mills rather than middlemen) but you'd have an inherently thin or negative margin on raw cotton production itself
The most realistic "ethical capitalist" path historically would probably be to skip raw cotton entirely and instead invest in the textile mills up north or in Britain — be the buyer of cheap Southern cotton rather than the grower. That's where the money was without needing slaves, and it's historically accurate too. The benefits of cotton produced by enslaved workers extended far beyond the South — in the North and Great Britain, cotton mills hummed, while the financial and shipping industries also saw gains. Banks in New York and London provided capital to new and expanding plantations.[7]

That would actually be a really interesting game mechanic — you can be the "clean hands" capitalist who profits indirectly from the system without owning a single slave, which is its own moral gray area.

Historians and economists have debated this exact question seriously for decades.This is actually one of the most historically rich and complex mechanics you could model, and real plantation owners were obsessed with this exact question. Here's the full picture:

The two competing management systems that actually existed

Real planters had two fundamentally different philosophies, and they argued about which was better constantly. Labor systems determined the pace, supervision, and community life for enslaved people, and two significant forms were the gang system and the task system.[1]

Gang System: The gang system grouped enslaved laborers to work from sunup to sundown under close supervision, with overseers, often armed, speeding up the work rate to ensure high yields.[1] This is the "stereotypical" plantation image. A plantation using gang labor could produce on average 39% more output from comparable inputs than free farms or farms employing non-gang labor — the secret was that slaves could be driven, leading to very profitable gangs supervised by white overseers and Black drivers.[9]

Task System: A slave was assigned a daily task to complete and allowed to do as they wished after it was finished. This was used throughout the South but was most common in the rice-growing areas of South Carolina, and frequently used on smaller farms.[4] This was the "gentler" approach — finish your quota, the rest of the day is yours.

Did better treatment actually improve productivity? Yes, but with a catch

After scouring old accounting books, historian Caitlin Rosenthal discovered that Southern planter-capitalists practiced an early form of scientific management — they took meticulous notes, carefully recording daily profits and productivity, and subjected their slaves to experiments and incentive strategies comprised of rewards AND brutal punishment.[5]

So the real answer isn't "nice vs. brutal" — it's that planters were constantly mixing both, like a modern manager using bonuses and performance reviews simultaneously. Plantation owners were generally convinced that slave labor was not highly efficient, and therefore believed that to produce crops profitably, slaves had to be worked long hours per day and many days per year.[7]

The resistance mechanic — and this is GOLD for your game

Here's what makes this genuinely fascinating from a game design angle. The "full brutal" approach had a serious hidden cost: resistance. Enslaved people often used the notion of paternalism to their advantage — some played into their masters' racism by hiding their intelligence and feigning childishness and ignorance, and could then slow down the workday and sabotage the system in small ways by "accidentally" breaking tools, which the master would believe were accidents rather than rebellions.[1]

It went further than just broken tools too. Enslaved plantation workers also engaged in coordinated work stoppages, slowdowns, and sabotage. Slaveholders often suspected complicity whenever a barn caught fire, a tool went missing, or a boiler exploded, though historians often struggle to distinguish enslavers' paranoia from actual organized resistance.[7]

And the nuclear option: the most common visible form of resistance was running away — fugitives found refuge in remote swamps and woods, and by hunting, foraging, and stealing from neighboring plantations, could live in relative freedom for days, months, or even years.[7]

So in pure game/spreadsheet terms, the model would look like this:

The "brutality" dial isn't a simple productivity slider. It's more like:

High brutality → higher raw output ceiling, but accumulating hidden "resistance" points that manifest as: tool breakage events, work slowdowns, escape attempts (losing your capital investment permanently), and at extreme levels, organized uprising risk
High paternalism → lower raw output, but lower resistance, lower escape rate, and workers who can be trusted with skilled tasks (carpentry, blacksmithing, driving other workers) which have higher value multipliers
There was even an informant mechanic — some enslaved people reported rebellious plans to their masters, hoping to gain preferential treatment, and those who informed could expect more lenient conditions.[2] So a "good treatment" run creates loyalty hierarchies within your workforce that act as an internal security system.
The truly dark and brilliant thing for your game is that there is no "good" settings — the task system just makes the horror feel more managerial and distant. The player optimizes either way, and the system is what's rotten, not just the dial position.

Yeah, "driven" is a very literal term — it's actually where the job title "driver" comes from, and it's one of the most fascinating and morally complex roles in the whole system.

Here's how the hierarchy actually worked on a big plantation:

At the top you had the owner, often not even present on the field. Below him was the overseer — usually a hired white man — who occupied a position in the managerial hierarchy between the owner and the Black drivers who directly supervised the work of their fellow slaves.[6]

Then there was the driver — and this is the dark twist. A driver was actually an enslaved person whose task was to command the gangs of field workers.[8] His function was to set the pace of work through a combination of example, cajolery, rewards, and punishments, including, if necessary, whipping, to see that the task at hand was accomplished.[4]

So the person literally cracking the whip to keep pace was often another enslaved person, not the white overseer. At its simplest, gang labor involved the supervised synchronic work of a group of field hands — a carefully selected worker set the pace, and the others were required to follow his example, urged on by the whip and yells of a white overseer or a black driver.[3]

The description was almost industrial. Contemporary observers like Frederick Law Olmsted described the process as "machine-like," with slaves exhibiting mechanical obedience amid constant surveillance.[1] Gang labor required the synchronized, mechanical performance of relatively simple, repetitive tasks at a high, regular work pace — in these respects, gang labor on U.S. plantations actually anticipated the regime of the industrial assembly line.[3]

For your game, the driver role is an incredibly rich mechanic. Drivers were part of the slave community, but were especially favored by the master — to maintain the goodwill of the master without losing the respect of fellow slaves was no small achievement, yet the evidence suggests drivers often succeeded where overseers failed.[7] So you'd have this character who is simultaneously a victim of the system AND an enforcer of it, with loyalty meters pulling in two directions at once. Unrealistic work demands from a driver might prompt a general flight to the swamps, sabotage, or worse[2] — meaning even the driver had to self-regulate how hard he pushed, because blowback landed on him too.

No need to apologize, it's a completely legitimate question and historians have actually studied this exact comparison pretty seriously.Really good question, and the historical answer is genuinely surprising — the white overseer on horseback with a whip is actually the less efficient and less trusted option in most cases. Here's the full breakdown:

The white overseer — costly, unreliable, and widely despised

Mississippi cotton plantation owners paid their overseer an average annual salary of about $450, with a range from $200 to $1,000, plus housing, a small garden, provisions, and possibly a horse.[3] That's a real ongoing operational cost on top of everything else.

But here's the kicker — planters hated their own overseers. If slaveholders were to be believed, their overseers were a low-bred class of scoundrels whose management swung erratically from violent abuse to lackadaisical incompetence.[1] And the data backed that up — plantation owners were rarely satisfied with their performance, constantly complained about them in letters and periodicals, and rarely kept the same one for more than a year or two.[1]

The structural reason for this is fascinating from a game design angle: with no direct proprietary interest in laborers, stock, or equipment and under constant pressure to maximize production, overseers had difficulty appreciating owners' concerns regarding the property that constituted the bulk of their capital investment.[7] In other words — the overseer had no skin in the game. He didn't own the slaves, so he didn't care if he broke them.

Some planters tried paying by commission to fix this incentive problem, but the obvious danger was that the planter might lose more due to the diminished value of his slaves through overwork than he gained by the large crop.[1]

The enslaved driver — cheaper, more knowledgeable, and often more trusted

Economic studies indicate that fewer than 30 percent of planters employed white supervisors for their slave labor.[10] That means the majority of plantations were being run day-to-day by enslaved drivers, not white overseers.

Why? Because drivers were often genuinely better at the job. Possessed of both practical and specialized knowledge of crop production, the slave driver could come to dominate field operations. Drivers were both generalists and specialists, but more than anything else they had to be "people managers" who knew how to set goals and motivate field laborers.[3]

The trust relationship also ran deeper than with white overseers. Masters often conferred with their black slave drivers on matters of farming and social arrangements, and often deferred to their advice. As the driver matured, his relationship with the master became one of mutual regard, in sharp contrast to the master's less settled and more transient relationship with white overseers.[5] Incredibly, a chorus of complaints from white Southern overseers alleged that planters trusted the black driver more than the overseer.[5]

The dark incentive structure of the driver

The reason drivers could be pushed to be brutal is deeply uncomfortable. Some slave proprietors, in order to save the cost of an overseer but chiefly to extract as much work as possible, made an enslaved person an overseer — who, if he did not cruelly work the slaves, was threatened with a flogging himself. In order to save his own back, the slave overseer very often behaved in the most brutal manner.[4]

So you had a coercion chain — the owner coerced the driver who coerced the workers, with each link enforcing the next under threat of violence.

So for your game, the spreadsheet comparison looks like this:

The white overseer is the expensive, low-loyalty, high-churn option — you pay a salary, he has no ownership stake, he'll likely over-brutalise your capital (the enslaved people), and he'll quit or be fired within a year or two. The enslaved driver costs nothing in salary, has deep crop knowledge, builds long-term relationships, but is himself trapped in the system and under pressure to brutalize his own community to protect himself. The "better" management option economically is morally the more horrifying one, because it makes the system self-enforcing from within.

Really good question and it's actually a whole industry unto itself, not just a single transaction. There were several completely different ways to acquire slaves, each with different price points, risks, and mechanics — which would all translate really well into your game.

The supply side first — where did slaves actually come from?

There are two very distinct eras here separated by a single law. In 1808, the importation of slaves to the United States from Africa was prohibited, and as a result, the price of slaves in the United States increased dramatically.[9] This created what historians call the domestic slave trade — essentially an internal American market.

From 1820 to 1860, over one million American slaves were sold in the domestic slave market. Most were sold by slaveholders in the Upper South states such as Virginia and Maryland, then transported and sold to plantation owners in the cotton-growing states of the Deep South such as Mississippi, Alabama, and Louisiana.[9]

So Virginia and Maryland were essentially slave-breeding and exporting states, and the Deep South cotton states were the buyers. Groups of slaves were transported by ship from places like Virginia — a state that specialized in raising slaves for sale — to New Orleans, where they were sold to planters in the Mississippi Valley.[9]

The ways you could actually buy

There were roughly four distinct purchase channels:

1. Private contract — the simplest mode of sale was by private contract, in which two or more individuals privately agreed on the price and terms of conveyance.[4] Basically a handshake deal between neighbors or acquaintances. No middleman, no fee.

2. Slave traders/brokers — a new class of businessmen arose who specialized in marketing, selling, housing, and transporting enslaved people on behalf of private owners. Private owners could contract with such agents, variously called brokers, auctioneers, or traders, who would make all the arrangements for a sale, including advertising, moving enslaved people from the country to the city, housing and feeding them in advance of the sale, and securing payment from bidders.[2] These were the professional middlemen of the trade.

3. Public auction — the most visible method. The auction block was a site of fear, humiliation, and uncertainty where loved ones were separated for life. Auction blocks could be found from colonial times well into the antebellum period, and were often seen in the public square and jail yards, at slave-trading offices, hotels, docks, and courthouses.[5] Interestingly, one historical argument is that roughly one-half of all slave sales were actually court-ordered or court-supervised auctions[1] — meaning the legal system itself was running the largest share of the market.

4. Estate sales — another large source of slave sales came from estate sales[5] — when a plantation owner died, his enslaved people were listed as assets and distributed or sold off with the rest of the estate.

The pricing system — and this is pure spreadsheet gold

Slaves were not priced arbitrarily. Slaves were graded for sale by skin color, height, weight, gender, and age.[2] There was even a formula tied directly to commodity prices: in 1858, the Wiregrass Reporter of Georgia advised estimating the value of enslaved people by a formula of "$100 for every cent per pound of cotton" — meaning if cotton was 10 cents per pound, you should pay $1,000 for an enslaved person.[6]

So slave prices were literally indexed to cotton prices in real time. If cotton boomed, slaves got more expensive. If cotton crashed, so did slave "values." That's a direct market linkage you could model in a game.

Skilled workers commanded massive premiums on top of that. A skilled enslaved mechanic would always sell for two to three times as much in the market as an unskilled field worker, and wealthy slave owners would vie with each other at auction and run the bidding up into high figures.[3]

The "slave pen" — the holding infrastructure

Slave pens, also known as slave jails, were used to temporarily hold enslaved people until they were sold, or to hold fugitive slaves, and sometimes even to "board" slaves while traveling.[3] These were essentially warehouses for human beings, and they were a whole separate business — hundreds of men in slave centers participated in the trade business, running hotels, auction houses, and slave jails and pens.[5]

Major slave-buying markets were located in Charleston, Savannah, Memphis, and above all, New Orleans. Economists estimate that more than 135,000 enslaved people were sold in New Orleans between 1804 and 1862.[6]

For your game, this means "buying slaves" is actually a whole mini-economy with distinct mechanics: you'd have a cotton price tracker that automatically adjusts slave prices, a skill tier system (field hand → driver → blacksmith → carpenter each at different price multipliers), multiple purchase channels with different costs and availabilities, and a seasonal market — in New Orleans, most sales were made between September and May[3] — because planting and harvest seasons were when nobody was buying or selling. That seasonality alone is a really authentic detail most games would never bother with.

This is genuinely one of the richest areas for your game design, because the soil question alone drives the entire macro-level expansion mechanic. Let me break it all down:

The Cotton Growing Calendar — it's basically year-round

The first thing to understand is that there was almost no true "off season." Cotton planting took place in March and April, when slaves planted seeds in rows around three to five feet apart. Over the next several months, from April to August, they carefully tended the plants and weeded the cotton rows. Beginning in August, all the plantation's slaves worked together to pick the crop, and cotton picking occurred as many as seven times a season as the plant continued to flower and produce bolls through the fall and early winter.[3]

So your labor calendar looks something like: Jan-Feb → soil prep, Mar-Apr → planting, May-Aug → weeding/tending, Aug-Dec → harvesting in multiple passes, and then immediately back to prep. Slaves used plows and hoes to turn the soil in the early months of the new year, removed old and dead stalks and fertilized the rows with ginned seed or guano imported from South America, and other slaves were ordered to mend the fences or plant corn and wheat in March, while in April laborers planted the seed by tilling it into the rows of soil.[1]

Actual farming techniques — and here's where it gets fascinating for your game

Irrigation: That was about the limit of cotton growing until you could pump underground water for irrigation.[1] In other words, antebellum cotton was almost entirely rain-fed. There was no meaningful irrigation system — you were entirely at the mercy of rainfall, which is a great weather/drought mechanic for your game.

Fertilizer: Planters were aware of the problem but had limited options. They fertilized the rows with ginned seed or guano, which was imported from South America.[1] Guano (bird droppings) was actually a major traded commodity in the 1800s — so you'd have a fertilizer supply chain that itself depends on international trade. Without the cattle and hogs of traditional farms, they had little natural fertilizer to restore the land, and artificial fertilizer was primitive before the Civil War.[3]

Crop rotation: This is where it gets really interesting — much of the South was planted in large monocultures, first tobacco and then cotton. Growing cotton and tobacco year after year takes the nutrients out of the soil. What could southerners do? With just one major crop, they could not shift from one crop to another as was done in the North.[3] Some progressive farmers such as Thomas Jefferson followed European models of fertilization, crop rotation, and livestock breeding, but most small farmers did not have the money to apply such techniques.[2]

The Soil Depletion Mechanic — this is the single most important mechanic for your game

This is huge. Chronic soil depletion left after less than five years exhausted, nutrient-depleted, largely unproductive dirt. Given the seemingly endless supply of new land and the lack of modern fertilizer, that problem never got solved in the antebellum period — soil depletion had to be included into the plantation business model.[2]

Many farmers in the South routinely abandoned their exhausted fields and moved further west with their slaves to virgin lands. Each cotton field hand typically wore out 30 to 50 acres during his or her lifetime, and shoddy conservation practices greatly diminished the value of southern agricultural land.[3]

To ambitious white planters, the extent of new land available for cotton production seemed almost limitless, and many planters simply leapfrogged from one area to the next, abandoning their fields every ten to fifteen years after the soil became exhausted.[4]

This is directly why the South politically demanded westward expansion — they needed fresh land to keep the cotton economy running. Planters moved west of the Appalachian Mountains, seeking new fertile lands in Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, and Texas, creating what historians call the "Cotton Kingdom." Federal policies like Indian Removal opened new territories, cotton prices remained high, and Western soil offered higher yields than the depleted soils of Virginia and the Carolinas.[6]

So your game has a built-in expansion pressure mechanic: your soil degrades over time, forcing you to either invest in expensive restoration (guano imports, crop rotation sacrificing cotton acres) or push west into new territory, which has its own political and logistical cost.

What slaves actually did when not picking cotton

The answer is: almost never idle. During agricultural off seasons, planters usually looked for ways to employ their idle slaves at a profit. They might hire their slaves out to the state or county to work on road construction crews or to builders as unskilled labor, and lumberjacking and mining were other jobs that required more muscle than skill.[1]

Slave owners could rent out their enslaved individuals during times of low demand or when they were not needed for plantation work, generating additional income, while businesses and individuals who needed labor could access it without having to invest in purchasing slaves.[2]

Skilled workers were especially valuable in this rental market. There were enslaved carpenters, weavers, plasterers, shoemakers, blacksmiths, tailors, and bricklayers in cities. These highly skilled artisans were valued by their enslavers and their communities, and they often enjoyed privileges and independence unavailable to most other enslaved people — they were typically subjected to less daily supervision and physical violence, and were often able to earn spending money by performing their trade after hours or because they were allowed to keep a portion of the wages they produced.[5]

And on-plantation, there was always internal work: masters and drivers allocated tasks based on experience, age, and gender, and unlike slaves on farms, those on plantations were assigned specialized tasks that included masonry, blacksmithing, and woodworking.[1]

For your game, the mechanics map out like this:

Every worker on your roster has a calendar-year utilization rate — during peak harvest they're all on cotton, but outside that you can assign them to: on-site construction (build new facilities), soil restoration work (recover degraded land slowly), hiring out (passive income but you lose local productivity), or skilled trades (blacksmith, carpenter, gin mechanic) which produce goods that either save you money or can be sold. The idle state essentially doesn't exist if you're managing efficiently, which is authentic and makes for deep gameplay.

Good, let's ground this properly. These are the four most important design decisions you'll make, so let me be direct about each one.

1. Starting in 1780 with Tobacco

This is actually a great call historically. Cotton doesn't even make economic sense until after 1793 when the cotton gin is invented, so starting with tobacco is accurate. The tutorial being tobacco management is smart too — tobacco is more labor-intensive to process (you have to cure it, age it, grade it), so it naturally teaches the player about multi-stage production before cotton simplifies things. Think of tobacco as the "hard mode" tutorial that makes cotton feel like a reward.

2. Time Cycle — go Seasonal (4 turns per year)

Monthly is too granular and will feel like a chore. Yearly is too slow and flattens the agricultural logic that makes the game interesting. Seasonal is the sweet spot because it maps perfectly onto the real farming calendar — Spring (plant), Summer (tend/weed), Fall (harvest), Winter (maintenance/planning). Each season you make decisions, then advance. That gives you roughly 4 meaningful decision points per year, which is the same cadence as something like Crusader Kings between events.

3. Research — don't call it research, call it "Adoption"

A traditional tech tree feels anachronistic and gamey for this setting. What actually happened historically is that technology became available and planters chose whether to invest in it. So your system should be:

Certain upgrades unlock automatically when the in-game year reaches their real historical date (the cotton gin unlocks in 1793, period)
But buying/adopting them costs money and has a transition cost (retraining workers, new infrastructure)
Some upgrades are purchasable anytime if you have capital — better plows, curing barns, a cotton gin of your own vs. paying to use someone else's
Knowledge improvements (better crop rotation, guano fertilizer) could come from hiring an "agricultural agent" or through random events like a neighbor demonstrating a new technique
This keeps it historical and grounded rather than feeling like a Civilization tech tree.

4. Worker System Depth — medium with one "deep" hook

For an HTML5 solo project, fully simulating individual workers is scope creep that will kill the project. The sweet spot is:

Workers are grouped by role (field hand, skilled trade, house worker, overseer) with productivity stats
Each group has aggregate health and morale that degrades or improves based on your decisions
But you have a small handful (maybe 5-10) of named individuals with specific skills — your blacksmith, your head overseer, your most experienced cotton picker — because that's where the moral weight actually lands for the player
The named individuals create emotional attachment AND give you a natural place to hang story events ("Thomas has been approached by an abolitionist network" is a much better event than "your field workers are restless").

5. HTML5 Libraries — yes, it's very much a thing

For a text-heavy management sim specifically, your options are:

Phaser.js — the biggest HTML5 game framework, but it's honestly overkill if you have no sprites or physics. Built for action/platformer style games.
Twine — if you want something closer to interactive fiction with game logic layered on top. Very fast to prototype in.
Vanilla JS + HTML/CSS — genuinely the right answer for a management sim. Your "game engine" is just DOM manipulation and a JavaScript data model. This is basically what Universal Paperclips is built on.
React — if you're already comfortable with it, a React app with state management is a perfectly legitimate way to build a management sim. GitHub Copilot will be very good at helping with this specifically.
Given you're moving to GitHub Copilot, I'd actually recommend vanilla JS or React over a dedicated game library, because Copilot is trained extremely well on both and you'll get much more useful suggestions than if you're working in Phaser's more niche API.

The Core Gameplay Loop in one sentence:

Each season, allocate your workers to tasks → resources are generated or consumed → market prices shift → you make financial decisions → soil/worker health degrades or recovers → repeat, with historical events and random crises punctuating the rhythm.

That's it. Everything else is depth layered on top of that spine.
